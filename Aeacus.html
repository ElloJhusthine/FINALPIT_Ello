Αἰακός

Aeacus

Aeacus is a significant figure in Greek mythology, known for his justice, piety, and eventually, his role as one of the three judges of the dead in the Underworld. Here’s a detailed account of Aeacus's story and the powers associated with him.

Aeacus was the king of the island of Aegina in Greek mythology. He was the son of Zeus and Aegina, daughter of the river god Asopus. Aegina was brought by Zeus to the island then called Oenone, in order to save her from her parents' wrath; the island later took after her name. Aeacus was born on the island, which was not inhabited at the time. So, Zeus transformed all the ants into men, forming the race of the Myrmidons (the word deriving from the Greek word for ant). Aeacus became the ruler of the Myrmidons. Another version has it that the island was inhabited; however, Hera, the jealous wife of Zeus, took the form of Aegina and sent a plague that drove away all of the inhabitants. That's when Zeus transformed the ants into humans.

Aeacus was considered a just and pious ruler, and was asked to judge over disputes of both men and gods all over Greece. The inhabitants of Aegina also believed that their ruler had created the cliffs that surrounded the island, in order to protect it from pirates. When Aeacus died, he went to the Underworld, where he became one of the three judges, the other two being Minos and Rhadamanthus.

The Story of Aeacus

Origins and Early Life

Aeacus was the son of Zeus, the king of the gods, and Aegina, a nymph and daughter of the river god Asopus. Zeus abducted Aegina and took her to the island of Oenone, later named Aegina in her honor, where Aeacus was born.

The Depopulation of Aegina

The island of Aegina was initially inhabited by a large population, but it was eventually struck by a plague (or, in some versions, a punishment by Hera, who was jealous of Aegina). The plague wiped out the entire population, leaving Aeacus as the sole survivor. In his despair, Aeacus prayed to his father, Zeus, to repopulate the island.

>

The Myrmidons

Zeus answered Aeacus’s prayer by transforming ants into humans, creating a new people for Aegina. These newly created inhabitants were called the Myrmidons, known for their loyalty, industriousness, and martial prowess. This story illustrates Aeacus’s close relationship with the gods and his importance in Greek mythology.

Reign as King

Aeacus became the king of Aegina and ruled with great wisdom and justice. His reign was marked by peace and prosperity, and he was highly respected for his fairness and piety. His reputation for justice was so great that even the gods sought his counsel in resolving disputes.

Aeacus’s Powers and Attributes

Justice and Fairness

Aeacus was renowned for his sense of justice and fairness. His ability to judge impartially and wisely made him a legendary figure in Greek mythology. This reputation extended beyond his mortal life.

Divine Favor and Communication

Aeacus had a close relationship with the gods, particularly Zeus. His prayers were answered by Zeus, and he was often involved in divine affairs. This connection to the divine granted him a status that few mortals could attain.

Judgeship in the Underworld

After his death, Aeacus was appointed as one of the three judges of the dead in the Underworld, alongside Minos and Rhadamanthus. In this role, he judged the souls of those who had passed, determining their fate in the afterlife. Aeacus was specifically responsible for judging the souls of Europeans. This role underscores his enduring legacy as a paragon of justice.

Wisdom and Piety

Aeacus was known for his wisdom and piety. His devotion to the gods and his adherence to moral principles made him an exemplary figure. His wisdom was sought by both mortals and gods, further solidifying his importance in Greek mythology.

Conclusion

Aeacus is a significant figure in Greek mythology, embodying the ideals of justice, piety, and wisdom. His story, from the depopulation of Aegina and the creation of the Myrmidons to his posthumous role as a judge in the Underworld, highlights his importance in the mythological canon. Aeacus's powers are not defined by physical might or magical abilities but by his moral virtues and his close relationship with the divine, making him a timeless symbol of justice and fairness.
